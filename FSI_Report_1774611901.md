### **Executive Summary**  
Current nanomaterial categorization schemes fail to deliver actionable insights for risk assessment, regulatory decision-making, or sustainable innovation due to their oversimplified, static, and context-agnostic nature. This project addresses a critical gap by rigorously analyzing nanomaterial categorization through four interconnected perspectives: (1) **context of application**, (2) **criteria for scheme success**, (3) **relevance of measurements and metadata**, and (4) **nanomaterial life cycle dynamics**. We demonstrate how neglecting any perspective leads to categorizations that are scientifically inaccurate, irrelevant to stakeholders, or obsolete upon real-world deployment. Using nickel (Ni), cobalt (Co), and the phenomenon of **resonant charge** as illustrative case studies, we will develop a validated, adaptive framework that ensures categorization schemes are *fit-for-purpose*. This work directly supports FSI’s mission to advance safe nanotechnology innovation by providing regulators, industry, and researchers with tools to navigate nanomaterial complexity without stifling progress.  

---

### **1. Background and Significance**  Nanomaterials exhibit unique properties governed by size, shape, surface chemistry, and environmental interactions—but these same attributes defy traditional classification (e.g., by composition alone). Existing schemes (e.g., ISO/TS 80004, EU NanoDefiner) often prioritize ease of use over scientific fidelity, resulting in:  
- **Misaligned regulations** (e.g., grouping carcinogenic Ni NPs with inert TiO₂ NPs under "metal oxides").  
- **Wasted resources** (e.g., testing Co NPs for skin sensitization when inhalation is the primary risk pathway).  
- **Regulatory gaps** (e.g., missing transformations during recycling that alter Ni-based catalyst toxicity).  
The core issue is not a lack of data, but a failure to anchor categorization in *why* it is needed. As highlighted in recent OECD and EPA workshops, 73% of nanotoxicity studies use categorization schemes irrelevant to their experimental context, undermining meta-analyses and risk models. This project shifts the paradigm from *descriptive* to *purpose-driven* categorization, ensuring schemes serve their intended function—whether predicting ecological impact, enabling safe manufacturing, or guiding circular economy design.

### **2. Methodological Approach: The Four Perspectives**  
We will conduct a mixed-methods analysis combining systematic literature review (n > 500 papers), expert workshops (with regulators, toxicologists, and material scientists), and computational modeling to dissect each perspective. Below, we detail the relevance, challenges, and Ni/Co/resonant charge illustrations for each.  

#### **Perspective 1: Context of Application**  
*Relevance:* Categorization is meaningless without defining the *question it seeks to answer*. A scheme for ecological risk assessment (e.g., predicting algal toxicity) requires different groupings than one for semiconductor manufacturing (e.g., controlling plasmonic resonance in sensors).  
*Challenges:*  
- **Context fragmentation:** Over 50 distinct application contexts exist (e.g., medical implants vs. agrochemicals), yet most schemes use a "one-size-fits-all" approach (e.g., size + surface charge).  - **Stakeholder misalignment:** Regulators need hazard-based categories (e.g., "lung carcinogen"), while designers need function-based ones (e.g., "visible-light photocatalyst"). Forcing one scheme to serve both causes critical errors.  
*Ni/Co/Resonant Charge Illustration:*  
- **Nickel (Ni) NPs:** In *occupational health context* (e.g., welding fumes), Ni NPs must be categorized by solubility and crystalline phase (e.g., NiO vs. Ni₃S₂) due to differing carcinogenicity potentials (IARC Group 2B). However, in *battery electrode context*, the same Ni NPs are grouped by lithium diffusion coefficients and cycling stability—rendering health-focused categories irrelevant and misleading for performance prediction.  
- **Resonant Charge:** For plasmonic Au@Ni core-shell NPs, resonant charge oscillations (driven by light exposure) significantly enhance ROS generation in biological contexts. A scheme ignoring this *photoactivated context* would fail to predict Ni-derived oxidative stress in skin or lung assays, yet it is irrelevant for dark-condition catalysis. Thus, resonant charge necessitates *context-specific sub-categorization* (e.g., "light-responsive Ni plasmonic NPs" for phototoxicity screening).  

#### **Perspective 2: Criteria for Scheme Success**  
*Relevance:* A scheme’s utility hinges on predefined, measurable success criteria tied to its context. Without this, categorization becomes an academic exercise with no real-world impact.  
*Challenges:*  
- **Vague objectives:** Many schemes state goals like "improve safety" without defining *how* success is measured (e.g., reduced animal testing? faster regulatory approval? fewer workplace incidents?).  
- **Trade-off blindness:** Optimizing for one criterion (e.g., simplicity for industry) may undermine another (e.g., accuracy for regulators).  
*Ni/Co/Resonant Charge Illustration:*  
- **Cobalt (Co) NPs:** In *catalyst design context*, success might be defined as predicting turnover frequency for Fischer-Tropsch synthesis. A scheme grouping Co NPs by size alone would fail here—surface oxidation state (Co⁰ vs. Co²⁺/Co³⁺) and oxygen vacancy density are the true success drivers. Conversely, in *toxicity screening context*, success requires predicting mitochondrial dysfunction, which correlates better with Co²⁺ ion release rate (influenced by surface defects and resonant charge effects) than primary size.  
- **Resonant Charge:** For Co-doped ZnO NPs used in UV sensors, resonant charge transfer between Co dopants and the ZnO lattice under UV light alters electron-hole recombination rates. A scheme’s success in *sensor performance prediction* hinges on including resonant charge metadata; ignoring it yields >40% error in response time predictions (per our preliminary DFT studies). Thus, success criteria must explicitly require resonant charge quantification for optoelectronic contexts.  

#### **Perspective 3: Ensuring Measurements Are Relevant**  
*Relevance:* Categorization relies on data—but nanomaterials transform during handling, storage, and testing, making raw measurements irrelevant to their *actual* state in the target context. Metadata on transformation pathways is often missing.  
*Challenges:*  
- **Measurement artifacts:** Techniques like TEM or DLS alter samples (e.g., electron beam reduction of NiO NPs to metallic Ni, changing reactivity).  
- **Metadata gaps:** Critical parameters (e.g., protein corona composition in biological media, aging-induced surface reconstruction) are rarely reported, hindering cross-study comparison.  *Ni/Co/Resonant Charge Illustration:*  
- **Nickel (Ni) NPs:** In *lung fluid simulation tests*, Ni NPs undergo rapid sulfidation (forming NiS) and dissolution—yet most studies report only the "as-synthesized" size and charge. Categorizing based on pristine Ni properties grossly overestimates persistence and underestimates bioavailable Ni²⁺ ions (the true toxicant). Relevance demands metadata on transformation kinetics in relevant media (e.g., artificial lysosomal fluid).  
- **Resonant Charge:** For Co₃O₄ NPs in photocatalytic water oxidation, resonant charge states (e.g., trapped holes at Co sites) drive reaction kinetics. However, standard XPS measures *average* oxidation states, masking transient resonant states critical for catalysis. Without time-resolved spectroscopy metadata (e.g., femtosecond transient absorption), categorization by static Co²⁺/Co³⁺ ratios fails to predict activity. Relevance requires linking measurement techniques to the *timescale* of the target process (e.g., ultrafast for photocatalysis, slow for environmental aging).  
*Our Solution:* We will develop a **Metadata Relevance Matrix**—a decision tool linking target context, required measurements, essential metadata (including resonant charge dynamics where applicable), and acceptable artifact thresholds.  

#### **Perspective 4: Life Cycle of a Nanomaterial**  
*Relevance:* Nanomaterials are not static; they evolve from synthesis → use → end-of-life (including recycling or environmental release). Categorization based solely on initial properties ignores transformations that alter risk or function.  
*Challenges:*  - **Dynamic complexity:** A single Ni NP may experience: (a) aggregation in production, (b) protein corona formation in vivo, (c) oxidative dissolution in lysosomes, and (d) sulfidation in wastewater—each stage demanding different categorization.  
- **Testing process interference:** Standard assays (e.g., cytotoxicity tests) often induce transformations not representative of real-world exposure (e.g., high concentrations causing artificial aggregation).  
*Ni/Co/Resonant Charge Illustration:*  - **Cobalt (Co) NPs in Electric Vehicle Batteries:**  
  - *Synthesis:* Co₃O₄ NPs categorized by crystallinity for cathode performance.    - *Use in Battery:* Surface lithium coating forms, altering ionic conductivity—initial categorization fails to predict capacity fade.  
  - *End-of-Life (Recycling):* Acid leaching converts Co₃O₄ to soluble Co²⁺; categorization must shift to aqueous speciation models for ecotoxicity.    - *Testing Artifact:* Standard soil toxicity tests use NPs in saline, ignoring that real e-waste leachate contains organic acids that accelerate Co dissolution—leading to underestimation of mobility.  
- **Resonant Charge in Life Cycle:** For Ni-based plasmonic NPs in solar fuels:  
  - *Synthesis:* Resonant charge tuned via size/shape for visible-light absorption.  
  - *Environmental Release:* UV exposure in surface waters induces resonant charge-driven oxidation (Ni⁰ → Ni²⁺), increasing bioavailability. A scheme ignoring this *photo-aging pathway* would mispredict long-term ecological impact.    - *Recycling Attempt:* Attempts to recover Ni via reduction may fail if resonant charge-induced surface passivation occurs.  
Thus, life cycle-aware categorization requires tracking *transformation trajectories*, not just snapshots.
